# All-Reduce Results Report

## Scope
This report summarizes what the charts show for the Part I all-reduce methods (naive, cube, MPI) and the Part II StackedReduce variant, based on the generated figures in [hw2/part1_total_runtime.png](hw2/part1_total_runtime.png), [hw2/part1_total_speedup.png](hw2/part1_total_speedup.png), [hw2/part1_total_efficiency.png](hw2/part1_total_efficiency.png), [hw2/part1_step2_runtime.png](hw2/part1_step2_runtime.png), [hw2/part1_step2_speedup.png](hw2/part1_step2_speedup.png), [hw2/part1_step2_efficiency.png](hw2/part1_step2_efficiency.png), [hw2/part2_total_runtime.png](hw2/part2_total_runtime.png), [hw2/part2_total_speedup.png](hw2/part2_total_speedup.png), [hw2/part2_total_efficiency.png](hw2/part2_total_efficiency.png), [hw2/part2_step2_runtime.png](hw2/part2_step2_runtime.png), [hw2/part2_step2_speedup.png](hw2/part2_step2_speedup.png), and [hw2/part2_step2_efficiency.png](hw2/part2_step2_efficiency.png).

## Part I: Naive vs Cube vs MPI
- Total runtime vs p is nearly flat for all three implementations once p > 1, indicating communication dominates and problem size is too small to benefit from more processes.
- Naive is consistently slower than cube and MPI for total runtime, while cube and MPI are similar and usually slightly faster.
- Speedup curves are far below 1 for p > 1 across all n, showing that parallel overhead outweighs any parallel work.
- Efficiency drops quickly with p and stays extremely low, matching the very small speedups.
- Step-2-only charts mirror the total runtime behavior, which means the reduction phase dominates total time for most n and p.

## Part II: StackedReduce
- Total runtime grows more noticeably with n than in Part I, especially at larger n, and stays roughly flat in p for most n.
- Speedup and efficiency are still far below 1 for p > 1, similar to Part I, with only small fluctuations across p.
- Step-2 runtime is effectively the same as total runtime for most n, indicating StackedReduce dominates overall time.
- A visible anomaly appears at n = 1,048,576 where p = 1 has a larger runtime than smaller n, causing a modest bump in speedup values.

## Justification
The results make sense given the expected behavior of an all-reduce dominated benchmark. For very small n, the single-process timing is tiny, so any communication cost with p > 1 dwarfs the compute time; this drives speedup below 1 and efficiency to near zero for all methods. The cube and MPI implementations are close because both rely on efficient communication patterns, while the naive method performs more explicit or less optimized communication, so its total time is higher. The flat runtime vs p curves suggest that the workload per process is too small to amortize MPI latency, and that the reduction step dominates total time (as seen by the Step-2 charts matching total runtime). In Part II, StackedReduce shows slightly higher runtimes and more growth with n, which is expected because the algorithm adds extra communication steps across stacked segments; this also keeps speedup low. The outlier at n = 1,048,576 for p = 1 likely reflects cache effects or measurement noise, which inflates speedup and efficiency at that point. Assumptions: results are based on the CSV/plots generated by [hw2/plot.py](hw2/plot.py), and timing is measured in seconds for total runtime (`t_total`) and all-reduce step (`t_step2`) as stored in the CSVs.
